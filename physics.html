Mechanics of Motion Mechanics
Q1Multiple Choice

Which scientist first described the concept of inertia as a property of matter at rest?

  • A Aristotle
  • B Galileo Galilei
  • C Isaac Newton
  • D René Descartes
✅ C — Isaac Newton. Newton formalized inertia as his First Law of Motion: an object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted on by an external force. Galileo challenged Aristotle's view that motion requires a continuous force, but Newton codified inertia into law.
Q2Multiple Choice

A car travels 100 m in 10 seconds. What is its average speed?

  • A 1000 m/s
  • B 10 m/s
  • C 0.1 m/s
  • D 100 m/s
✅ B — 10 m/s. Average speed = distance ÷ time = 100 m ÷ 10 s = 10 m/s. Speed is a scalar — it only tells you how fast, not which direction. If the question asked for velocity, you'd also need to specify the direction of travel.
Q3Multiple Choice

Newton's Third Law states that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Which of the following is the best example of this?

  • A A rocket expelling gas downward and moving upward
  • B A ball rolling to a stop due to friction
  • C A car accelerating when the engine applies force
  • D A book resting on a table
✅ A — A rocket. The rocket pushes exhaust gas backward (action); the gas pushes the rocket forward (reaction). The forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different objects — the hallmark of Newton's Third Law.
Q4Multiple Choice

Two forces act on an object: 3 N to the right and 4 N upward. Using the graphical (tip-to-tail) method, what is the magnitude of the resultant force?

  • A 7 N
  • B 1 N
  • C 5 N
  • D 12 N
✅ C — 5 N. When two perpendicular vectors are added graphically (tip-to-tail), the resultant is the arrow drawn from the tail of the first to the tip of the last. For a 3-4-5 right triangle, the resultant = 5 N. You can verify this with a ruler and protractor on a scale drawing.

Momentum & Impulse Mechanics
Q5Computation

A 2 kg ball moving at 6 m/s collides with a wall and comes to rest. What impulse did the wall exert on the ball?

  • A 6 N·s
  • B 12 N·s
  • C 3 N·s
  • D 0 N·s
✅ B — 12 N·s. Impulse = change in momentum = mΔv = 2 × (0 − 6) = −12 N·s. The magnitude is 12 N·s. Impulse equals the area under a force-time graph and always equals the change in momentum.
Q6Multiple Choice

Two ice skaters push off each other from rest. Skater A (60 kg) moves left at 2 m/s. What can we say about Skater B?

  • A Skater B also moves left
  • B Skater B remains stationary
  • C Skater B moves right — total momentum stays zero
  • D Skater B moves right with the same speed as A
✅ C. Since the system started at rest, total momentum = 0. After the push, Skater A has −120 kg·m/s (left). Skater B must have +120 kg·m/s (right) to keep the total at zero. Skater B's speed depends on their mass — same speed only if masses are equal.

Gravitation Gravity
Q7Multiple Choice

Which scientist published the law of universal gravitation in 1687?

  • A Galileo Galilei
  • B Isaac Newton
  • C Johannes Kepler
  • D Tycho Brahe
✅ B — Isaac Newton. Newton published his law of universal gravitation in Principia Mathematica (1687): F = Gm₁m₂/r². Kepler described planetary orbits mathematically before Newton, and Tycho Brahe collected the observational data Kepler used — but Newton unified it all into one law.
Q8Multiple Choice

If the distance between two masses is doubled, the gravitational force between them becomes:

  • A Doubled
  • B Halved
  • C Unchanged
  • D One quarter of the original
✅ D — One quarter. Gravity follows an inverse square law: F ∝ 1/r². If r doubles, r² quadruples, so F becomes 1/4 of the original. This is why gravity weakens rapidly with distance — the relationship is not linear, it's squared.
Q9Multiple Choice

A ball is thrown upward. At the highest point of its trajectory, which of the following is true?

  • A Velocity is zero but acceleration is still 9.8 m/s² downward
  • B Both velocity and acceleration are zero
  • C Velocity is upward, acceleration is downward
  • D Velocity is zero, acceleration is zero
✅ A. At the peak, the ball momentarily stops (v = 0) but gravity never stops acting. Gravitational acceleration is always 9.8 m/s² downward — it does not "turn off" at the highest point. This is a very common misconception.

Thermodynamics Thermo
Q10Multiple Choice

Heat transfer through direct contact between particles of matter is called:

  • A Radiation
  • B Convection
  • C Conduction
  • D Induction
✅ C — Conduction. Conduction occurs when heat flows through direct particle-to-particle contact — like a metal spoon heating up in soup. Convection transfers heat through fluid movement (currents). Radiation transfers heat via electromagnetic waves, requiring no medium at all.
Q11Multiple Choice

The Sun heats the Earth across the vacuum of space. Which mode of heat transfer makes this possible?

  • A Conduction
  • B Radiation
  • C Convection
  • D All three equally
✅ B — Radiation. Space is a vacuum — there is no medium for conduction (requires contact) or convection (requires fluid). Radiation travels as electromagnetic waves (infrared, visible light) and needs no medium, making it the only method that works across space.

Wave Mechanics Waves
Q12Multiple Choice

A transverse wave is one where the particle vibration is:

  • A Perpendicular to the direction of wave travel
  • B Parallel to the direction of wave travel
  • C Circular around the direction of travel
  • D Stationary while the wave moves
✅ A — Perpendicular. In a transverse wave, particles move up and down (or side to side) while the wave travels forward — like a wave on a string. Longitudinal waves (like sound) have particles vibrating parallel to the direction of travel in compressions and rarefactions.
Q13Computation

A wave has a frequency of 200 Hz and a wavelength of 1.7 m. What is its speed?

  • A 200 m/s
  • B 117.6 m/s
  • C 340 m/s
  • D 1.7 m/s
✅ C — 340 m/s. Wave speed v = fλ = 200 × 1.7 = 340 m/s. This is approximately the speed of sound in air at room temperature — a useful value to remember.

Light & Optics Waves
Q14Multiple Choice

Which scientist first demonstrated that white light is composed of all colors of the spectrum?

  • A James Clerk Maxwell
  • B Thomas Young
  • C Christiaan Huygens
  • D Isaac Newton
✅ D — Newton. Newton used a prism to disperse white light into its spectrum of colors and then used a second prism to recombine them — proving white light is not pure but is made of all visible wavelengths. Young later proved the wave nature of light through his double-slit experiment.
Q15Multiple Choice

When light passes from air into glass (a denser medium), it:

  • A Speeds up and bends away from the normal
  • B Slows down and bends toward the normal
  • C Maintains the same speed and direction
  • D Reflects completely back into air
✅ B. When light enters a denser medium, it slows down and bends toward the normal line (the line perpendicular to the surface). This is refraction. The opposite happens when leaving a dense medium — it speeds up and bends away from the normal.
Q16Multiple Choice

A concave mirror can produce a real, inverted image. Which type of mirror always produces a virtual, upright, and smaller image?

  • A Convex mirror
  • B Concave mirror
  • C Plane mirror
  • D Both concave and convex
✅ A — Convex mirror. A convex (diverging) mirror always produces a virtual, upright, and smaller image — regardless of where the object is. This is why they're used for wide-angle views in car side mirrors and security cameras. Plane mirrors produce virtual, upright, same-size images.

Electrostatics & Circuits Electric
Q17Multiple Choice

Who discovered that electric and magnetic phenomena are related, unifying them into electromagnetism?

  • A Benjamin Franklin
  • B André-Marie Ampère
  • C James Clerk Maxwell
  • D Michael Faraday
✅ C — Maxwell. James Clerk Maxwell formulated the four equations that unified electricity, magnetism, and light into one theory of electromagnetism (1865). Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction experimentally; Ampère studied the magnetic effects of current; but Maxwell created the complete mathematical framework.
Q18Multiple Choice

Two objects are charged by rubbing. One gains electrons; the other loses them. The one that loses electrons becomes:

  • A Positively charged
  • B Negatively charged
  • C Neutral
  • D Magnetically charged
✅ A — Positively charged. Electrons carry negative charge. Losing electrons means losing negative charge, leaving an excess of positive (protons), making the object net positive. The object that gains electrons becomes negatively charged. Charge is always conserved — what one gains, the other loses.
Q19Computation

A circuit has a voltage of 12 V and a resistance of 4 Ω. What is the current flowing through it?

  • A 48 A
  • B 0.33 A
  • C 8 A
  • D 3 A
✅ D — 3 A. Ohm's Law: I = V/R = 12/4 = 3 A. Voltage (V) is the push; Resistance (Ω) is the opposition; Current (A) is the flow. Always identify the two known values and solve for the third.
Q20Multiple Choice

Electromagnetic induction is the process of generating an electric current by:

  • A Applying a constant voltage to a wire
  • B Moving a conductor through a changing magnetic field
  • C Rubbing two conductors together
  • D Connecting a battery to a resistor
✅ B. Faraday's Law: a changing magnetic flux through a conductor induces an EMF (and therefore a current). This is the principle behind generators, transformers, and wireless charging. A static magnetic field induces nothing — it must be changing.
