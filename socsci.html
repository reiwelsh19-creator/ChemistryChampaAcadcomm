Quarter 1 — Multiple Choice

Which of the following best describes why early human civilizations tended to develop near rivers?

  • A Rivers provided natural barriers against enemy armies
  • B Rivers supplied fresh water, fertile soil, and transportation routes
  • C Rivers were considered sacred, attracting religious communities
  • D Rivers were the only source of fish in the ancient world
✅ B. Early civilizations needed reliable water for agriculture, fertile floodplain soil for crops, and rivers as trade and communication routes. The Tigris-Euphrates, Nile, Indus, and Yellow River all supported these needs — making river valleys the cradles of civilization.

The term "Fertile Crescent" refers to a region in the ancient Middle East that was the birthplace of which civilization?

  • A Egyptian civilization along the Nile
  • B Indus Valley civilization in South Asia
  • C Mesopotamian civilization between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers
  • D Chinese civilization along the Yellow River
✅ C. The Fertile Crescent is the arc-shaped region of the Middle East, centered on Mesopotamia (modern Iraq), between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. It is often called the "cradle of civilization" — home to the Sumerians, who developed one of the world's first writing systems (cuneiform) and legal codes (Code of Hammurabi).

Which feature MOST distinguishes a civilization from a simpler society?

  • A Complex social organization, specialized labor, and a system of writing
  • B The use of fire and basic tools
  • C Nomadic movement across large territories
  • D Belief in a single god
✅ A. Historians identify civilizations by several key characteristics: urban centers, complex social hierarchies, specialized occupations, organized government, writing systems, long-distance trade, and monumental architecture. The shift from nomadic hunter-gatherers to settled, organized societies marks the rise of civilization.

The ancient Greeks made significant contributions to Western civilization. Which of the following is a Greek contribution?

  • A The invention of the zero and decimal system
  • B The construction of the Great Wall to protect their empire
  • C The development of the caste system
  • D Democracy, philosophy, drama, and the Olympic Games
✅ D. Ancient Greece gave Western civilization the concept of democracy (Athens under Cleisthenes), philosophy (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle), theater (tragedy and comedy), the Olympic Games, and advancements in mathematics and science. Zero and decimals originated in South Asia; the Great Wall is Chinese; the caste system is Hindu/Indian.

The Maya civilization is best known for which achievements?

  • A Building the Colosseum and Roman aqueducts
  • B Developing a complex writing system, accurate calendar, and advanced mathematics
  • C Constructing the pyramids of Giza along the Nile
  • D Establishing the Silk Road trade network
✅ B. The Maya of Meso-America developed one of the few fully developed writing systems of the pre-Columbian Americas (hieroglyphs), a solar calendar more accurate than the European calendar of that time, and advanced mathematics including the concept of zero. They built impressive cities like Chichen Itza and Tikal in the jungles of present-day Mexico and Guatemala.
Quarter 2 — Multiple Choice

The Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path are central teachings of which religion?

  • A Hinduism
  • B Islam
  • C Buddhism
  • D Christianity
✅ C. Buddhism was founded by Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha) in the 5th century BCE in ancient India. The Four Noble Truths describe the nature of suffering (dukkha) and its causes, while the Eightfold Path provides the way to end suffering and achieve nirvana. Buddhism spread across Asia and today has hundreds of millions of followers worldwide.

Which of the following BEST describes Islam's core declaration of faith?

  • A "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is his messenger."
  • B "Love your God with all your heart and love your neighbor as yourself."
  • C "The middle path leads to enlightenment and the end of suffering."
  • D "Dharma, karma, and reincarnation govern the cycle of life."
✅ A. The Shahada — "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is his messenger" — is the first and most important of the Five Pillars of Islam. Reciting it with sincere belief makes one a Muslim. The other choices reflect Christian, Buddhist, and Hindu/general South Asian religious concepts respectively.
Quarter 3 — Empire Building & Middle Ages

Ashoka the Great transformed from a conquering emperor to a ruler guided by Buddhist principles primarily because of:

  • A A dream that warned him of divine punishment if he continued to wage war
  • B Pressure from his adviser Kautilya, who wrote the Arthasastra
  • C Witnessing the massive death and suffering caused by his conquest of Kalinga
  • D A treaty imposed by the Gupta Empire that required non-violence
✅ C. The Battle of Kalinga (c.261 BCE) resulted in over 100,000 deaths. Ashoka was so horrified by the carnage he had caused that he converted to Buddhism and adopted a policy of dhamma — ruling by Buddha's teaching of "peace to all beings." He inscribed new policies of non-violence and religious tolerance on stone pillars across his empire. His transformation is one of history's most remarkable — from conqueror to peace-builder.

The Mandate of Heaven in Chinese political thought meant that:

  • A A ruler's right to govern depended on ruling justly — and could be lost if they failed the people
  • B Only descendants of the gods could become emperor of China
  • C Heaven literally wrote the name of each new ruler on a heavenly scroll
  • D Chinese emperors had absolute, unquestionable authority from birth to death
✅ A. The Mandate of Heaven (Tianming) was a political concept that justified both obedience to a ruler and rebellion against a corrupt one. A good ruler who brought peace and prosperity had the Mandate. A ruler who overtaxed, neglected infrastructure, or allowed corruption lost it — signaled by floods, revolts, and invasions. This concept powered the Dynastic Cycle and was used for over 2,000 years to explain why dynasties rose and fell.

Which of the following BEST explains why the Mongol Empire was called a "paradox" — both destructive and beneficial?

  • A The Mongols destroyed cities but always rebuilt them more beautifully
  • B Mongol conquests caused massive destruction, but the Pax Mongolica enabled safe trade, cultural exchange, and the spread of ideas across Eurasia
  • C Mongols were cruel warriors but converted everyone they conquered to Buddhism
  • D They destroyed Western civilization but preserved Eastern civilization
✅ B. Genghis Khan's campaigns wiped out entire populations. Yet once the empire was established, the Pax Mongolica ("Mongol Peace") imposed order across Eurasia — allowing merchants, missionaries, and travelers safe passage from China to Europe. Inventions like gunpowder and papermaking traveled westward; silk and spices flowed eastward. The Black Death also spread along these same trade routes. The Mongols adopted the cultures of conquered peoples rather than imposing their own.

The Japanese called the typhoon that destroyed Kublai Khan's invasion fleet "Kamikaze." What does Kamikaze mean?

  • A "Ocean serpent"
  • B "Warrior spirit"
  • C "Emperor's blessing"
  • D "Divine winds"
✅ D. In 1281, Kublai Khan sent 150,000 warriors to invade Japan. A powerful typhoon destroyed the fleet. The Japanese, believing the gods had protected them, called the storm Kamikaze — "Divine Winds." Japan sits in the Sea of Japan, known for strong seasonal typhoons. The term Kamikaze was later used in WWII for Japanese suicide pilots who flew planes into Allied warships — invoking the same spirit of divine sacrifice.

The Ottoman Empire's Devshirme System involved:

  • A Granting autonomy to religious minorities to manage their own communities
  • B Requiring Muslim subjects to pay a special tax for army service
  • C Forcibly conscripting Christian boys, converting them to Islam, and training them as elite Janissary soldiers or administrators
  • D Sending Ottoman princes to conquered territories to learn local cultures
✅ C. The Devshirme ("collection") System took Christian boys from conquered Balkan territories as a tribute. They were converted to Islam, given rigorous training in combat, military science, and administration. The most successful became Janissaries — the empire's elite military force — or high-ranking government officials. Paradoxically, these men, taken from their families as children and made slaves, could rise to the highest levels of Ottoman power and wealth.

What made Angkor Wat remarkable as a religious structure?

  • A It is the largest religious structure in the world, initially dedicated to Vishnu and later used as a Buddhist temple — demonstrating syncretism
  • B It was the first structure ever built using concrete
  • C It was built entirely underground to protect it from invaders
  • D It served exclusively as the burial site for Cambodian royalty for 500 years
✅ A. Angkor Wat (12th century, Cambodia) is the largest religious structure in the world — covering approximately 400 acres. It was originally built as a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu and also served as an astronomical observatory. After the Khmer Empire adopted Buddhism, Angkor Wat transitioned into a Buddhist temple — a perfect example of Southeast Asian syncretism, the blending of Hindu and Buddhist traditions. It remains Cambodia's national symbol and appears on its flag.
Quarter 3 — Reformation & Early Modern

Martin Luther's 95 Theses (1517) were primarily a protest against which practice of the Catholic Church?

  • A The translation of the Bible into Latin
  • B The appointment of popes by the Holy Roman Emperor
  • C The church's refusal to allow priests to marry
  • D The sale of indulgences — payments to reduce time in purgatory
✅ D. Luther's 95 Theses directly challenged the Catholic Church's practice of selling indulgences — documents that promised to reduce a sinner's time in purgatory. Luther argued that salvation came through faith alone (sola fide) and that the Bible, not the Pope or church tradition, was the ultimate authority (sola scriptura). This sparked the Protestant Reformation.

The Columbian Exchange refers to:

  • A The trade of gold and silver between Spain and Portugal
  • B The transfer of plants, animals, diseases, and culture between the Old and New Worlds after 1492
  • C Christopher Columbus's agreement with the Spanish crown for funding his voyage
  • D The exchange of enslaved Africans for European manufactured goods
✅ B. The Columbian Exchange, named after Christopher Columbus, refers to the widespread transfer of plants (potatoes, tomatoes, corn going East; wheat, sugar going West), animals (horses going West; pigs, cattle going East), diseases (smallpox devastated Native American populations), and people between the Eastern and Western hemispheres after 1492. It fundamentally transformed diets, economies, and populations worldwide.

Which Enlightenment thinker argued that all people are born with natural rights to "life, liberty, and property"?

  • A Jean-Jacques Rousseau
  • B Voltaire
  • C John Locke
  • D Baron de Montesquieu
✅ C. John Locke (1632–1704) argued in his Two Treatises of Government that all individuals possess natural rights to life, liberty, and property — rights that no government can rightfully remove. His ideas directly influenced the American Declaration of Independence (Jefferson changed "property" to "pursuit of happiness") and the French Revolution. Rousseau emphasized the "social contract"; Montesquieu championed separation of powers; Voltaire championed religious tolerance.
Quarter 4 — Multiple Choice

The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand in 1914 triggered World War 1 because:

  • A Europe's system of alliances pulled multiple nations into conflict through a "chain reaction"
  • B Ferdinand was the ruler of Germany, and his death was an act of war
  • C The assassination directly destroyed the treaty that kept Europe at peace
  • D The United States immediately declared war on Austria-Hungary
✅ A. Franz Ferdinand was the heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, not the ruler of Germany. His assassination by a Bosnian-Serb nationalist triggered a chain reaction through Europe's complex alliance system: Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia; Russia mobilized to defend Serbia; Germany declared war on Russia; France (allied to Russia) entered; Germany invaded Belgium, pulling in Britain. The MAIN causes of WWI are remembered as Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, and Nationalism.

The Cold War (1947–1991) was primarily a conflict between:

  • A The United States and China over control of the Pacific
  • B NATO and the United Nations over global peacekeeping
  • C Western Europe and Eastern Europe over territorial borders
  • D The United States (capitalism/democracy) and the Soviet Union (communism) over ideology and global influence
✅ D. The Cold War was an ideological and geopolitical rivalry between the US-led capitalist democratic West and the Soviet-led communist East. It was called "cold" because the two superpowers never directly fought each other militarily — instead, they competed through proxy wars (Korea, Vietnam), the Space Race, the Arms Race, and spreading their respective systems of government to newly independent nations. The Soviet Union dissolved in 1991, marking the end of the Cold War.
Short Answer / Identification

What is the name of the ancient region between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, often called the "cradle of civilization"?

✅ Mesopotamia — from the Greek words meaning "land between the rivers." Located in modern-day Iraq, it was home to the Sumerians, Babylonians, and Assyrians. The Code of Hammurabi, the Epic of Gilgamesh, and cuneiform writing all originated here.

What are the five central duties of every Muslim, which form the foundation of Islamic practice?

✅ The Five Pillars of Islam: (1) Shahada — declaration of faith; (2) Salah — praying five times daily; (3) Zakat — giving to charity; (4) Sawm — fasting during Ramadan; (5) Hajj — pilgrimage to Mecca at least once in a lifetime if able. These form the core framework of a Muslim's religious life.

What is the name of the hierarchical political and social system that dominated medieval Europe, where land was exchanged for military service and loyalty?

✅ Feudalism — The medieval European system where kings granted land (fiefs) to nobles (lords) in exchange for military service and loyalty. Lords in turn granted land to knights and serfs worked the land. The hierarchy went: King → Lords/Nobles → Knights → Serfs/Peasants. The Catholic Church was also a powerful institution at every level.

What is the name of the cultural and intellectual movement that began in 14th-century Italy, characterized by a renewed interest in classical Greek and Roman learning, humanism, and the arts?

✅ The Renaissance — French for "rebirth." It began in Italian city-states like Florence and Venice in the 1300s–1400s, fueled by wealthy patrons (the Medici family), the rediscovery of ancient texts, and a new focus on humanism — placing humans, rather than God, at the center of intellectual inquiry. Key figures include Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, and Galileo.

What international organization was founded in 1945 after World War 2 to promote peace, security, and cooperation among nations?

✅ The United Nations (UN) — Founded in October 1945 by 51 countries, with headquarters in New York City. It replaced the failed League of Nations. The UN's main organs include the General Assembly (all member states), the Security Council (5 permanent members with veto power: US, UK, France, Russia, China), and the Secretariat. The Philippines was a founding member.
Study Guide · World History
Historical Timeline & Key Terms
Quick reference for each quarter's major events and concepts
🏺 Quarter 1 — Prehistory & Ancient World
c. 200,000 BCE
Emergence of Homo Sapiens
Modern humans evolve in Africa. Early humans live as hunter-gatherers, using fire and basic stone tools. The Paleolithic (Old Stone Age) spans from about 2.5 million years ago to 10,000 BCE.
c. 10,000 BCE
Neolithic Revolution — Agriculture Begins
Humans transition from nomadic hunter-gathering to settled farming (the Neolithic or Agricultural Revolution). Permanent villages form, leading to population growth, surplus food, and eventually civilization.
c. 3500–3000 BCE
The Four River Civilizations Rise
Mesopotamia (Tigris-Euphrates), Egypt (Nile), Indus Valley, and ancient China (Yellow River) develop independently as the world's first complex urban civilizations — each with writing, government, and monumental architecture.
c. 800–400 BCE
Classical Greece — The Golden Age
Athens establishes democracy under Cleisthenes. Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle lay the foundations of Western philosophy. The Parthenon is built. The Persian Wars and the Peloponnesian War shape Greek history.
✝️ Quarter 2 — Religions & Belief Systems
c. 2000 BCE onward
Judaism — The First Monotheistic Religion
Abraham's covenant with God marks the beginning of Judaism. The Torah contains laws and history. Moses leads the Israelites out of Egypt. The Ten Commandments are given at Mount Sinai. Judaism deeply influenced both Christianity and Islam.
c. 563–483 BCE
Buddhism — The Middle Way
Siddhartha Gautama, a prince in present-day Nepal, renounces wealth to seek the end of suffering. After deep meditation under the Bodhi tree, he attains enlightenment and becomes the Buddha ("the Awakened One").
c. 4 BCE – 30 CE
Christianity — Life and Teachings of Jesus
Jesus of Nazareth preaches in the Roman province of Judea. His teachings of love, forgiveness, and the Kingdom of God attract followers. After his crucifixion and claimed resurrection, his disciples spread Christianity throughout the Roman Empire.
610 CE onward
Islam — Muhammad's Revelations
Muhammad receives revelations from Allah (God) through the angel Gabriel in Mecca. These revelations form the Quran. Muhammad flees to Medina in 622 CE (the Hijra — Islamic calendar begins here). Islam spreads rapidly across Arabia, Persia, and North Africa.
🏺 Quarter 3 — Asian & Muslim Empires
322–185 BCE
Mauryan Empire — Ashoka's Transformation
Chandragupta Maurya unifies northern India. His grandson Ashoka conquers Kalinga, horrified by 100,000 deaths, converts to Buddhism, and rules by non-violence — inscribing policies on stone pillars. Adviser Kautilya wrote the Arthasastra, an early guide to statecraft.
320–535 CE
Gupta Empire — India's Golden Age
Art, mathematics, and science flourish. Aryabhata calculates pi to 4 decimal places and approximates the solar year. Physicians document 1,000 diseases. Chandra Gupta II opens Mediterranean trade. Falls to Hun invasions in 535 CE.
221 BCE–960 CE
Chinese Dynasties: Qin through Song
Qin Shi Huang unifies China via Legalism, building the Great Wall and Terracotta Warriors. Han Dynasty (400+ years) thrives through Confucianism and Silk Road trade. Tang Dynasty's golden age: Empress Wu Zetian, expansion, foreign trade. Song Dynasty: gunpowder, movable-type printing, compass — inventions that transformed the world.
1206–1368 CE
Mongol Empire — Genghis to Kublai Khan
Temujin unites Mongol clans (1206) and becomes Genghis Khan. The empire stretches from Korea to Poland — the largest contiguous land empire in history. Four khanates divide it after his death. Pax Mongolica enables Silk Road trade. Kublai Khan's Yuan Dynasty rules China; his 1281 Japan invasion is destroyed by a Kamikaze typhoon.
1299–1707 CE
The Three Muslim Empires: Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal
Ottoman Empire: Controls Middle East, N. Africa, E. Europe; Suleyman the Magnificent's golden age; Millet and Devshirme systems. Safavid Persia: Shia Islam as state religion; Abbas the Great's cultural golden age in Esfahan. Mughal India: Babur founds the gunpowder empire; Akbar's religious tolerance; Shah Jahan builds the Taj Mahal; Aurangzeb's intolerance accelerates decline.
802–1511 CE
Southeast Asian Empires — Khmer, Majapahit, Malacca
Khmer Empire builds Angkor Wat — the world's largest religious structure. Majapahit rules Java as the last Hindu-Buddhist kingdom. Ayutthaya (Siam) becomes Thailand's trading powerhouse. Malacca Sultanate controls the spice trade until Portuguese conquest in 1511 — marking European imperialism's arrival in Southeast Asia.
⚔️ Quarter 3 — Middle Ages & Early Modern Europe
476 CE – c. 1500
The Middle Ages in Europe
The fall of the Western Roman Empire ushers in the Medieval period. Feudalism organizes society. The Catholic Church is the dominant political and spiritual force. The Crusades (1095–1291) are military campaigns to reclaim the Holy Land. The Black Death (1347–1351) kills one-third of Europe's population.
1300s–1600s
The Renaissance — "Rebirth" of Knowledge
Beginning in Italian city-states, the Renaissance sees renewed interest in classical learning, humanism, and artistic achievement. Key figures: Leonardo da Vinci (art, science), Michelangelo (Sistine Chapel), Gutenberg (printing press, c.1440), Copernicus (heliocentric model).
1517
Protestant Reformation — Luther's Challenge
Martin Luther posts his 95 Theses, challenging the Church's sale of indulgences. He argues for sola fide (faith alone) and sola scriptura (Bible alone). This fractures Western Christianity into Catholic and Protestant branches. John Calvin and Henry VIII further divide the Church.
1400s–1600s
Age of Exploration — Maritime Revolution
Portuguese and Spanish sailors sail beyond Europe. Vasco da Gama reaches India (1498). Christopher Columbus lands in the Americas (1492). Ferdinand Magellan's expedition completes the first circumnavigation of the globe (1519–1522). This opens the age of European colonialism.
1687–1789
Scientific Revolution & the Enlightenment
Newton's laws of motion and universal gravitation transform science. Enlightenment thinkers — Locke (natural rights), Montesquieu (separation of powers), Rousseau (social contract), Voltaire (religious freedom) — reshape political thought. Their ideas fuel the American (1776) and French (1789) Revolutions.
🌍 Quarter 4 — The Modern World
1914–1918 & 1929–1939
World War 1 and the Great Depression
WWI (the "Great War") kills 17 million people. The Treaty of Versailles (1919) punishes Germany harshly, sowing seeds of WWII. The Great Depression begins with the Wall Street Crash (1929), causing mass unemployment worldwide and fueling the rise of fascism in Europe.
1939–1945
World War 2 — The Deadliest Conflict
Hitler's Nazi Germany invades Poland, starting WWII. The Holocaust kills 6 million Jews and 5 million others. The Allies (US, UK, USSR, France) eventually defeat the Axis powers. The atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki end the war in the Pacific. The Philippines suffers greatly during Japanese occupation.
1947–1991
The Cold War
The US and USSR compete for global influence without direct warfare. Key events: Berlin Blockade (1948), Korean War (1950–53), Cuban Missile Crisis (1962), Vietnam War (1955–75), Space Race, and the fall of the Berlin Wall (1989). The USSR dissolves in 1991, ending the Cold War.
1945–1990s
Decolonization — Asia and Africa Gain Independence
Dozens of colonies achieve independence after WWII weakened European powers. India (1947), Indonesia (1945), the Philippines (1946), Ghana (1957), and many African nations gain self-rule. Leaders like Mahatma Gandhi (India) and Kwame Nkrumah (Ghana) lead independence movements.
1945–Present
Globalization and International Organizations
The UN (1945), World Bank, IMF, WTO, and ASEAN (1967) form to promote cooperation. Globalization — the growing interconnection of economies, cultures, and information — accelerates with the internet age. The world faces shared challenges: climate change, terrorism, pandemics, and inequality.
