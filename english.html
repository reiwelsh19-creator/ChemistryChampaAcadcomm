📝 Grammar: Subject-Verb Agreement
01 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
The news about the discoveries in the Antarctic        scientists around the world.
✓ Fascinates — "The news" is singular even though it sounds plural. News, mathematics, economics, physics, and similar words ending in -s are singular nouns and take singular verbs.
02 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
Neither the principal nor the teachers        aware of the schedule change.
✓ Were — With neither…nor / either…or, the verb agrees with the subject closest to it. "Teachers" (plural) is closest, so use the plural verb "were."
03 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
Every student and teacher in the school        expected to attend the assembly.
✓ Is — When "every" or "each" precedes a compound subject joined by "and," the verb is singular. "Every student and teacher" = singular concept.
📝 Grammar: Verbals
04 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
Identify the type of verbal in the underlined word: "Running every morning improves cardiovascular health."
✓ Gerund — "Running" functions as the subject of the sentence ("Running… improves"). When a word ending in -ing acts as a noun (subject, object, or complement), it is a gerund.
05 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
Which sentence contains a DANGLING modifier?
✓ Option C — "Exhausted after the hike" dangles because it has no clear subject to modify. The tent cannot be exhausted. The sentence should read: "Exhausted after the hike, they set up the tent quickly."
📝 Grammar: Sentence Errors
06 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
Identify the type of error: "The movie was incredible, it had stunning visuals and a powerful soundtrack."
✓ Comma splice — Two complete independent clauses ("The movie was incredible" + "it had stunning visuals…") are joined with only a comma. Fix: use a semicolon, a period, or add a conjunction — "The movie was incredible; it had stunning visuals and a powerful soundtrack."
07 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
Which of these is a sentence FRAGMENT?
✓ Option C is a fragment — it begins with a subordinating conjunction ("Because") but has no independent clause to complete the thought. It leaves the reader asking: "What happened because the power went out?"
📝 Logical Fallacies
08 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
"You can't trust Mark's opinion on climate change — he failed his science class last year." This is an example of:
✓ Ad hominem — attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. Even if Mark failed science class, his current claims about climate change must be evaluated on their own merits.
09 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
"If we allow students to use phones during lunch, soon they'll be on them all day and never learn anything." This is:
✓ Slippery slope — assumes one action will inevitably lead to a chain of negative consequences without evidence that this progression is likely or necessary.
10 / MULTIPLE CHOICE
"You're either with us or against us." This statement commits which fallacy?
✓ False dichotomy (false dilemma) — presents only two options when more exist. In reality, someone could be neutral, partially supportive, or opposed for complex reasons.
📐 Grammar Reference
Subject-Verb Agreement — Key Rules
RuleExample
Nouns that look plural but are singular (news, mathematics, physics)The news is alarming. Mathematics is useful.
Collective nouns are usually singularThe committee has made its decision.
Neither…nor / Either…or → verb agrees with nearest subjectNeither she nor they were ready.
Every / Each + compound subject → singular verbEvery boy and girl is welcome.
Indefinite pronouns: everyone, someone, nobody → singularEveryone has a role to play.
Intervening phrases don't affect agreementThe box of chocolates is on the table.

Modifier Errors
Misplaced Modifier
Placed too far from what it modifies — creates ambiguity.

✗ She almost drove her children to school every day.
✓ She drove her children to school almost every day.
Dangling Modifier
Has no clear noun to modify in the sentence.

✗ Walking to school, the rain began to fall.
✓ Walking to school, I got caught in the rain.
Consistency in Writing (Q3) — All Five Types
TypeRuleInconsistent ✗Consistent ✓
PersonDon't shift between 1st, 2nd, 3rd person without reasonWhen one exercises, you feel better.When one exercises, one feels better.
Point of ViewKeep the narrative perspective stable throughoutI walked in. Suddenly she saw the note.I walked in. Suddenly I saw the note.
NumberDon't shift between singular and plural for the same nounA student should submit their assignment on time.Students should submit their assignments on time.
VoiceDon't mix active and passive voice unnecessarilyShe wrote the report and then it was submitted.She wrote the report and then submitted it.
TenseDon't shift tense unless the time actually changesHe opens the door and walked inside.He opened the door and walked inside.
Verbals — Deep Dive
Gerund — acts as a noun
Form: verb + -ing

Subject: Running every morning improves stamina.
Object: She enjoys reading classic novels.
Complement: His hobby is hiking.
Infinitive — noun, adj, or adverb
Form: to + base verb

Noun: To succeed requires patience.
Adjective: She has a plan to solve it.
Adverb: He studied hard to pass.
Participle — acts as adjective
Form: -ing / -ed / irregular

Present: The student sitting up front answered.
Past: Exhausted by the run, he collapsed.
⚠️ Watch out — participial phrase at the start must modify the subject:
✗ Running to catch the bus, the rain started. (rain wasn't running)
✓ Running to catch the bus, she got soaked by the rain.

Punctuation rule: [Independent clause] ; conjunctive adverb , [independent clause]
✗ I was tired, however, I kept working.  |  ✓ I was tired; however, I kept working.

✍️ Essay Writing Guide
Compare & Contrast Essay (Q1)
Block Method
Discuss Subject A completely, then Subject B completely. Best for shorter essays or very different subjects.
Point-by-Point Method
Alternate between subjects for each point of comparison. Best for complex comparisons with many shared criteria.
Similarity Transitions
similarly · likewise · in the same way · just as · both · also
Contrast Transitions
however · on the other hand · in contrast · whereas · while · unlike · although · conversely
Problem-Solution Essay (Q3)
1
Introduction — Hook (startling fact / statistic / anecdote) → Background → Thesis (names the problem and hints at solution)
2
Problem Paragraph(s) — Define the problem, its causes, its effects, evidence of its severity
3
Solution Paragraph(s) — Propose the solution, explain HOW it works, address objections, explain feasibility
4
Conclusion — Restate problem + solution, broader significance, call to action
✗ Weak: "The government should do something about poverty."
✓ Strong: "Schools should implement mandatory 30-minute reading periods, as research shows this increases literacy rates by 40%."
Argumentative Essay (Q4)
Logos
Logic and evidence — facts, statistics, data, expert quotes. Most important in academic writing.
Ethos
Credibility — using reliable, authoritative sources and demonstrating knowledge of the topic.
Pathos
Emotional appeal — connecting to the audience's values, experiences, or feelings about the issue.
1
Introduction — Hook → Background → Thesis (clear, specific, debatable)
2
Body Paragraphs — Topic sentence → Evidence → Explanation/Analysis → Transition
3
Counterargument — Acknowledge opposing view → Concede valid points → Refute with stronger evidence
4
Conclusion — Restate thesis differently → Synthesize main points → Broader implication / call to action

Key Logical Fallacies
Ad Hominem
Attacking the person rather than their argument.
Straw Man
Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack.
False Dichotomy
Presenting only two options when more exist.
Slippery Slope
Claiming one action will inevitably cause extreme consequences.
Hasty Generalization
Drawing a broad conclusion from too little evidence.
Circular Reasoning
Using the conclusion as evidence for itself.
Appeal to Authority
Citing an authority figure whose expertise is irrelevant.
Bandwagon
Arguing something is true because many people believe it.
