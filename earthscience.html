Earth Structure & Tectonics Q1
Q1Multiple Choice

Which layer of the Earth is responsible for driving the movement of tectonic plates through convection currents?

  • A Inner core
  • B Mantle
  • C Crust
  • D Outer core
✅ B — The mantle. The mantle is a semi-solid layer of hot rock that flows very slowly. Heat from the core causes convection currents in the mantle — hot rock rises, spreads, cools, and sinks — dragging tectonic plates along the surface. The outer core is liquid iron but drives Earth's magnetic field, not plate motion directly.
Q2Multiple Choice

Lines of latitude run parallel to the equator. What is the latitude of the North Pole?

  • A 0°
  • B 45° N
  • C 90° N
  • D 180°
✅ C — 90° N. Latitude measures angular distance from the equator (0°) to the poles (90° N or S). Longitude measures east-west position from the Prime Meridian (0°) to 180°. The Philippines sits at roughly 13° N latitude, 122° E longitude.
Q3Multiple Choice

At a divergent plate boundary, two plates move apart. What geological feature commonly forms here at mid-ocean ridges?

  • A New oceanic crust from rising magma
  • B Deep ocean trenches
  • C Mountain ranges through collision
  • D Transform fault earthquakes only
✅ A — New oceanic crust. At divergent boundaries, plates pull apart and magma wells up from the mantle, solidifies, and creates new seafloor — a process called seafloor spreading. The Mid-Atlantic Ridge is a classic example. Trenches form at convergent (subduction) zones; mountains at continent-continent collisions; transform faults produce lateral slip earthquakes.
Q4Multiple Choice

What type of volcano is characterized by alternating layers of lava and pyroclastic material, producing steep-sided cones like Mt. Mayon?

  • A Shield volcano
  • B Cinder cone
  • C Caldera
  • D Composite (stratovolcano)
✅ D — Composite volcano (stratovolcano). Stratovolcanoes are built from alternating layers of hardened lava and ash/pyroclastic material, giving them their steep, symmetrical shape. Mt. Mayon in the Philippines is the world's most nearly perfect cone. Shield volcanoes (like Mauna Loa) are broad and flat from fluid lava. Cinder cones are small and loose-material only.

Eruptions, Earthquakes & Weathering Q2
Q5Multiple Choice

Which type of volcanic eruption is the most explosive, producing large amounts of ash and pyroclastic flows?

  • A Hawaiian eruption
  • B Plinian eruption
  • C Strombolian eruption
  • D Fissure eruption
✅ B — Plinian eruption. Named after Pliny the Younger who described the eruption of Mt. Vesuvius (79 CE), Plinian eruptions are catastrophically explosive — high-silica magma traps gas and explodes violently, sending ash columns tens of kilometers high. Hawaiian eruptions are gentle and effusive (fluid lava). Strombolian are moderate. The 1991 eruption of Mt. Pinatubo in the Philippines was Plinian.
Q6Multiple Choice

The point inside the Earth where an earthquake originates is called the:

  • A Epicenter
  • B Seismograph
  • C Focus (or hypocenter)
  • D Fault scarp
✅ C — Focus (hypocenter). The focus is the underground origin point where rock first ruptures. The epicenter is the point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus — this is where shaking is usually strongest. Seismographs are the instruments used to detect and record seismic waves.
Q7Multiple Choice

The Philippine Archipelago sits at the intersection of several tectonic plates. Which tectonic setting primarily explains its high volcanic and seismic activity?

  • A It lies along the Pacific Ring of Fire at subduction zones
  • B It sits on a divergent mid-ocean ridge
  • C It formed from a continental collision like the Himalayas
  • D It formed from a hotspot like the Hawaiian Islands
✅ A — The Ring of Fire / subduction zones. The Philippines sits at the convergence of the Philippine Sea Plate, the Eurasian Plate, and others. Oceanic crust subducts beneath the archipelago, generating magma that fuels its many volcanoes (Mayon, Pinatubo, Taal) and creating frequent earthquakes. The Philippines has over 300 volcanoes, about 24 of which are active.
Q8Multiple Choice

A limestone cave forms when slightly acidic rainwater slowly dissolves the rock. This is an example of:

  • A Physical (mechanical) weathering
  • B Erosion by water
  • C Biological weathering
  • D Chemical weathering
✅ D — Chemical weathering. Chemical weathering changes the mineral composition of rocks through reactions. Carbonation (CO₂ + H₂O = carbonic acid) dissolves limestone — this creates caves, sinkholes, and karst landscapes. Physical weathering breaks rocks into smaller pieces without changing their chemistry (e.g. freeze-thaw, abrasion). Erosion is the transport of weathered material.

Geological Time, Water & Weather Q3
Q9Multiple Choice

The principle of superposition states that in undisturbed rock layers:

  • A The top layer is always the oldest
  • B All layers are the same age
  • C The bottom layer is the oldest and the top layer is the youngest
  • D Layers alternate between old and young
✅ C — Bottom is oldest, top is youngest. The principle of superposition is the foundation of relative dating: sedimentary layers are deposited sequentially, so older layers lie beneath younger ones in undisturbed sequences. An unconformity is a gap in the rock record where erosion removed layers before new deposition resumed.
Q10Multiple Choice

About what percentage of Earth's water is fresh water available for human use (not frozen in ice caps)?

  • A 50%
  • B Less than 1%
  • C About 3%
  • D About 10%
✅ B — Less than 1%. About 97% of Earth's water is saltwater in oceans. Of the 3% that is fresh, about 2% is locked in ice caps and glaciers. Less than 1% is liquid fresh water in lakes, rivers, groundwater, and the atmosphere — the water humans and most terrestrial life depend on.
Q11Multiple Choice

Which layer of the atmosphere contains the ozone layer that shields Earth from ultraviolet radiation?

  • A Stratosphere
  • B Troposphere
  • C Mesosphere
  • D Thermosphere
✅ A — Stratosphere. The atmosphere's layers from lowest to highest: Troposphere (weather occurs here; 0–12 km), Stratosphere (ozone layer; 12–50 km), Mesosphere (meteors burn up; 50–80 km), Thermosphere (auroras; 80–700 km), Exosphere (outer fringe). Temperature decreases with altitude in the troposphere but increases in the stratosphere due to ozone absorbing UV.
Q12Multiple Choice

A tropical cyclone (typhoon) requires which of the following conditions to form and intensify?

  • A Cold ocean water below 10°C
  • B Strong vertical wind shear
  • C Dry upper atmosphere
  • D Warm ocean water (at least 26–27°C) and high humidity
✅ D — Warm ocean water and high humidity. Typhoons are fueled by latent heat released when warm, moist air rises and water vapor condenses. Ocean surface temperatures of at least 26–27°C are needed. Strong wind shear disrupts their structure; cold water cuts off their energy source. The Philippines experiences an average of 20 typhoons per year due to its location in the western Pacific.

The Celestial Sphere Q4
Q13Multiple Choice

The celestial equator is defined as:

  • A The path the Sun takes across the sky throughout the year
  • B The projection of Earth's equator onto the celestial sphere
  • C The line dividing the northern and southern constellations
  • D The plane of Earth's orbit around the Sun
✅ B — Projection of Earth's equator onto the sky. The celestial sphere is an imaginary sphere surrounding Earth onto which all celestial objects are projected. The celestial equator is Earth's equator extended to this sphere. The ecliptic (the Sun's apparent yearly path) is tilted 23.5° from the celestial equator — the same as Earth's axial tilt.
